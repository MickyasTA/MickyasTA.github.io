DRL collision avoidance

Vision-based, goal-directed quadrotor navigation learned end-to-end with a from-scratch TD3 from stacked depth frames — the agent learns obstacle avoidance and goal seeking together, trained in Gazebo Classic on ROS 2 Humble with no external RL libraries.

🔒 Code is private — available on request. This page describes the project at a high level; if you want to talk details, get in touch.

What it is

A quadrotor has to reach a goal point in a cluttered room — red boxes, grey pillars, L-shaped walls — without hitting anything, using only what it can see. There is no map and no planner: a single neural-network policy reads depth vision and a goal direction and outputs continuous velocity and yaw commands, learning to route around obstacles on its way to the goal.

How it works

The observation is a 7-channel 128×160 tensor — six stacked depth frames (clipped to 0–10 m) plus a goal-direction channel — alongside an explicit goal vector [sinθ, cosθ, 1 − d/10] fed to the actor and critic heads. The policy commands continuous velocity and yaw-rate. Reward is dominated by progress toward the goal, plus a heading-alignment term and a small survival bonus, minus a collision penalty; collision, goal, and stuck are terminal while timeout is deliberately non-terminal, since the agent cannot observe time.

The learner is a TD3 written from scratch in PyTorch — twin critics, target-policy smoothing, and delayed actor updates — with exploration noise annealed from 40% down to 10% over training. A discrete D3QN agent is included as an alternative.

Curriculum that respects the room

Goal distance grows with the agent's success rate, and a per-room feasibility probe caps it so the curriculum never asks for a goal the world cannot actually place. Training spans nine worlds, from trivial to expert, orchestrated across runs because Gazebo Classic cannot hot-swap worlds.

Results

On the hardest goal tier of gen_room_mixed, the deterministic (noise-off) policy reaches ~80% success with near-optimal paths (1.05× the straight-line distance) and 0% timeouts — it commits to the goal rather than stalling. Goal-generation failures are detected and excluded from the success rate, so the reported numbers are honest.

Honest evaluation

Every number comes from a deterministic harness — greedy policy, no exploration noise, no training, no curriculum advance, no weight writes — that logs trajectories and turns them into a trajectory map, statistics panel, failure analysis, animated playback, a third-person Gazebo render, and a written report. The point is a success rate you can trust, not a best-case cherry-pick.
