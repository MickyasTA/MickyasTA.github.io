mybot — wheel-legged bipedal robot

A two-wheeled, wheel-legged bipedal robot that learns to balance on its wheels and drive to a goal point — trained with a from-scratch PPO (no Stable-Baselines3, no RL library), written from the equations up in PyTorch.

🔒 Code is private — available on request. This page describes the project at a high level; if you want to talk details, get in touch.

What it is

mybot has two independent legs (a 3-DOF hip plus knee each) ending in driven wheels. With its centre of mass above the wheel axle it is statically unstable — standing still is already a control problem, exactly like a Segway. The legs are individually actuated revolute joints, so once balance is solved they can be added to the policy for squatting, stepping, and climbing.

How it learns

The controller is a hand-written PPO: an actor-critic MLP with a learnable log-std, generalized advantage estimation, a clipped surrogate objective, and running mean/std normalization on both observations and rewards — all in PyTorch, no RL library. Control runs at 50 Hz. The single most important stabilizer was a KL cap (--target-kl 0.02): without it KL blew up to 0.3–1.0, the policy over-updated and degraded; with it, training converges smoothly to a full 1000-step balance.

  • Observation — base roll/pitch and angular rate, wheel velocities, base velocity, leg positions/velocities and the previous action (69-d with a 3-frame history at Stage 1; 117-d at Stage 2 once all eight leg joints are exposed).
  • Action — two wheel torques (±20 Nm) at Stage 1; a 10-D action adding eight leg position targets at Stage 2.
  • Reward — an alive bonus minus penalties on tilt, x-y drift, yaw-rate and effort. The yaw-rate term matters: without it the robot balances but spins in place (~230°/s); with it, it holds heading to within a few degrees.

One policy, a curriculum

Each stage warm-starts from the previous policy. Stage 1 (balance) reaches a full 20-second balance. Stage 2 (goal navigation) unlocks the legs as a real 8-D action and adds a base-frame goal vector to the observation, with reward terms for progress toward the goal, facing it, arriving, and station-keeping there. A first attempt exploited a loophole — splaying the legs into a wide static split for free lateral stability — so a posture penalty was added to keep the legs near their stance. Actuating the legs, adding a goal, and banning the shortcut all at once made the robot fall in a few steps, so the stage was split into 2a (balance with actuated legs, no goal) then 2b (warm-start 2a, add the goal), which learns navigation while keeping its balance from step one.

Results

  • Balance — holds the full 1000-step (20 s) episode upright on its wheels.
  • Goal navigation — 9/10 held-out episodes arrive and station-keep, mean end-distance 0.16 m from a mean start of 1.47 m, still balancing the full episode.

Two simulators, one hard lesson

Training runs primarily in MuJoCo, with a ROS 2 Humble + Gazebo Classic stack kept for integration and reference. The split was forced by physics: Gazebo Classic could not hold this robot's load-bearing, chained revolute leg joints — they buckle to a collapsed pose the instant physics runs and then kinematically lock (confirmed across every actuator, gain, and damping setting). MuJoCo's constrained dynamics hold the same joints at 0.007 rad under full body load, so training moved there. On this machine a parallel-CPU PPO (N MuJoCo envs across cores, plain multiprocessing, still no RL library) trained the goal-nav result ~5–10× faster than a single env, while a JAX/MJX GPU path is kept as a flag for larger machines.

Status

  • Done — Stage 1 balance and Stage 2 goal navigation, verified on held-out episodes.
  • Next — obstacle avoidance, ducking and terrain (squat under obstacles, stairs, rough ground), then sim-to-real with IMU-only observations and domain randomization.
