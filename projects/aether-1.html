AETHER-1

A hybrid legged-wheeled-aerial robot controlled by one reinforcement-learning policy that balances, drives, flies, lands, and — most importantly — decides for itself when to drive and when to fly, because flying costs roughly ten times more energy than driving.

🔒 Code and full technical documentation are private while the hardware program and intellectual-property work are in progress. This page describes the project at a high level; if you want to talk details, get in touch.

Why a hybrid?

Inspection robots today split into two families with hard limits: confined-space drones fly superbly but only for minutes, and ground robots run for hours but stop at the first stairwell, drop, or debris field. The physics is unforgiving — hovering burns kilowatts while driving burns watts. AETHER-1's answer is one asset that drives for hours and spends its precious flight seconds only where wheels cannot go, with that trade-off learned through an energy-priced reward rather than hard-coded.

How it works

One brain, grown through a curriculum. A single policy, trained from scratch in MuJoCo, controls the whole robot. It earns its skills in stages — balance, driving, hover, 3D flight, and finally free drive-or-fly arbitration — and advancement is never scheduled: a stage is passed only when measured success rates clear its gate. Earlier skills are continually rehearsed while new ones are learned, so the policy that eventually flies has not forgotten how to balance — anti-forgetting by construction, not by hope. The final stage closed at a 0.995 success gate under domain randomization.

Hybrid by design, not by fallback. The learned policy doesn't replace classical control — it is composed with it. LQR balance and model-based flight controllers act as stabilizing bases on which the policy learns residual corrections, as independent safety layers, and as honest benchmarks: learned, classical, and hybrid controllers are interchangeable behind one interface and compared on identical seeded evaluations, so every claim about "the learned controller is better here" is a measured one.

Decisions that emerge, training that polices itself. The robot is never told when to fly — the fly-vs-drive choice emerges from a reward that prices energy, so the policy discovers on its own that wheels are cheap and rotors are precious. And because reward-driven learning fails quietly, the training run polices itself: physics sanity gates, an extensive automated test suite, and a reward-economics audit that halts training if any stage ever makes failure more profitable than success.

Results

Before anything is cleared for hardware, the deployed controller must pass a seeded, deterministic verification battery in MuJoCo — 18 scenarios covering driving, flight, landing, a domain-randomization sweep, and edge cases, measured on the exact controller build that would run on the robot. The training run itself closed its final curriculum stage at a 0.995 success gate; the battery below is the independent check on the deployed build, and it currently stands at 14 PASS / 4 WARN / 0 FAIL with no divergence in any episode.

From simulation to hardware

The 26.47 kg simulation robot is the validated dynamics reference; the hardware track is a 15 kg Mk1 demonstrator with a full engineering package behind it: component selection and sourcing, power and endurance modeling, EU regulatory strategy, a parametric CAD model with a validated URDF, staged test rigs that replace guessed simulation constants with measured ones, and a safety-gated bring-up procedure. The physics that motivated the project stays the design driver: driving is roughly ten times cheaper than hovering, so the hybrid spends rotor time only where wheels cannot go — the same energy asymmetry the policy learned to exploit in simulation.

Status

  • Done — simulation-validated full stack (curriculum complete at 0.995), deployment package with hard sim verification, Mk1 engineering package (BOM, build plan, CAD + URDF).
  • Now — Mk1 hardware prototype program: test rigs, procurement, build.
  • Next — tethered flight tests, the drive→fly→land→drive demo, and first pilot conversations in industrial inspection.
